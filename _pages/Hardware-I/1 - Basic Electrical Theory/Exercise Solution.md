# Exercise Solutions: Chapter 1 - Basic Electrical Theory

*Note: These solutions are provided for practice and understanding. They are not part of the actual exam syllabus.*

## Multiple Choice Questions

### Question 1: An ohm meter is used to measure
**Answer: a. Resistance**

**Explanation:** An ohm meter is specifically designed to measure electrical resistance. It sends a small current through the component and measures the voltage drop to calculate resistance using Ohm's law.

### Question 2: A voltmeter is used to measure
**Answer: b. Voltage**

**Explanation:** A voltmeter measures the potential difference (voltage) between two points in an electrical circuit. It must be connected in parallel across the component whose voltage is being measured.

### Question 3: An ammeter is used to measure
**Answer: c. Current**

**Explanation:** An ammeter measures the flow of electric current through a circuit. It must be connected in series with the circuit to measure the current flowing through it.

### Question 4: In series connection following remains the same
**Answer: c. Current**

**Explanation:** In a series circuit, the same current flows through all components because there is only one path for current to flow. The voltage divides across the components, but current remains constant throughout.

### Question 5: In parallel connection following remains the same
**Answer: b. Voltage**

**Explanation:** In a parallel circuit, all components share the same voltage across them because they are all connected to the same two points. The current divides among the different branches.

## Short Answer Questions

### Question 1: Define Electricity.

**Answer:** Electricity is the flow of electrical energy. Electricity is the secondary form of energy which means that other sources of energy such as kinetic energy, thermal energy and solar energy are converted into electrical energy.

### Question 2: How is electricity transmitted from power plants to your homes?

**Answer:** Electricity is generated at dams and thermal power plants located in remote areas. The electricity that power plants generate is delivered to consumers over transmission and distribution power lines. This complex system sometimes called the "grid" includes substations, transformers, and power lines that connect electricity producers and consumers.

The process involves:
- Generation at power plants (25,000V)
- Step-up transformers increase voltage to 138,000-765,000V for transmission
- Long-distance transmission lines carry high voltage electricity
- Substations step voltage down to 4,000-35,000V for distribution
- Local transformers reduce voltage to 120-240V for household use

### Question 3: Define Voltage, Resistance and Current.

**Answer:**

**Voltage:** A voltage is a potential difference between two points of an electric circuit. A voltage is the pressure from an electrical power source that causes current in an electric circuit. It is represented by V and measured in volts (V).

**Current:** Flow of charges inside an electric circuit is called electric current. An electric power source such as a battery consists of high number of charged particles which creates a potential difference between two points of an electric circuit, causing current. It is represented by I and measured in amperes (A).

**Resistance:** A resistance is a component opposing the current flow in an electric circuit. The higher the value of resistance, more will be the opposition. It is represented by R and measured in ohms (Ω).

### Question 4: What is the difference between AC and DC quantities?

**Answer:**

**Direct Current (DC):**
- Magnitude and direction of DC remains constant
- Current flows in one direction only
- Examples: batteries, solar panels, electronic devices

**Alternating Current (AC):**
- AC keeps on changing its direction periodically
- Voltage varies sinusoidally over time
- Examples: household electricity, power grid

The main difference is that DC flows in one constant direction while AC alternates direction periodically.

### Question 5: Differentiate between conductors, insulators and semiconductors.

**Answer:**

**Conductors:**
- Current can flow in conductors easily
- Have free electrons on their surface which allow current to pass through
- Made up of atoms having free electrons
- Examples: Copper, silver, aluminum

**Insulators:**
- Made up of material that do not allow electricity to pass through
- Made up of atoms that do not have free electrons
- Examples: Plastic, glass, rubber

**Semiconductors:**
- Have limited free electrons
- Conductivity lies between conductors and insulators
- Examples: Silicon, germanium

## Detailed Answer Questions

### Question 1: Differentiate between electric power and electric energy.

**Answer:**

**Electric Power:**
- Electric power is the rate at which electrical energy is consumed or produced
- It is measured in watts (W)
- Formula: P = VI = I²R = V²/R
- It represents how fast energy is being used at any instant
- Example: A 60W bulb uses 60 watts of power continuously

**Electric Energy:**
- Electric energy is the total amount of electrical work done over a period of time
- It is measured in kilowatt-hours (kWh)
- Formula: Energy = Power × Time
- It represents the total consumption over time
- Example: A 60W bulb running for 10 hours consumes 0.6 kWh of energy

**Key Difference:** Power is the rate of energy consumption (instantaneous), while energy is the total amount consumed over time.

### Question 2: Describe Ohm meter.

**Answer:**

An ohm meter is a device used to measure electrical resistance. The key features are:

**Construction:**
- Has two test leads (probes) for connection
- Contains an internal voltage source
- Has a display to show resistance values
- May be analog (needle) or digital

**Working Principle:**
- Applies a small known voltage across the component
- Measures the resulting current
- Calculates resistance using Ohm's law (R = V/I)

**Usage:**
- Connect test leads to both ends of the resistor
- Ensure circuit is powered off before measurement
- Read the resistance value on the display
- Results displayed in ohms (Ω), kilohms (kΩ), or megohms (MΩ)

### Question 3: Describe Ammeter.

**Answer:**

An ammeter is an instrument used to measure electric current flowing through a circuit.

**Key Characteristics:**
- Always connected in series with the circuit
- Has very low internal resistance to avoid affecting circuit current
- Measures current in amperes (A), milliamperes (mA), or microamperes (μA)

**Construction:**
- Contains a galvanometer as the basic measuring element
- May have multiple ranges for different current levels
- Can be analog (moving coil) or digital

**Connection Method:**
- Circuit must be broken to insert ammeter
- Current flows through the ammeter
- Must observe polarity (positive to positive terminal)

**Safety Considerations:**
- Never connect ammeter in parallel (can cause short circuit)
- Start with highest range and work down
- Ensure ammeter rating exceeds expected current

### Question 4: Describe Voltmeter.

**Answer:**

A voltmeter is an instrument used to measure electrical potential difference (voltage) between two points in a circuit.

**Key Characteristics:**
- Always connected in parallel across the component
- Has very high internal resistance to avoid drawing current from circuit
- Measures voltage in volts (V), millivolts (mV), or kilovolts (kV)

**Construction:**
- Contains a galvanometer with high series resistance
- May have multiple voltage ranges
- Can be analog (moving coil) or digital

**Connection Method:**
- Connected across the component without breaking the circuit
- Positive probe to higher potential point
- Negative probe to lower potential point
- Measures potential difference between the two points

**Applications:**
- Measuring battery voltage
- Checking voltage drops across components
- Testing power supply outputs
- Troubleshooting electrical circuits

### Question 5: Differentiate between cells and batteries.

**Answer:**

**Cell:**
- A cell is a single unit device that converts chemical energy into electrical energy
- Typical voltage rating: 1.5V or 3.7V
- Contains one anode, one cathode, and electrolyte
- Cannot be subdivided into smaller units
- Examples: AA cell, AAA cell, coin cell

**Battery:**
- A battery is a group of cells connected together
- Voltage depends on number and arrangement of cells
- Can be formed by connecting cells in series or parallel
- Can be subdivided into individual cells
- Examples: Car battery (6 cells), laptop battery (multiple cells)

**Connection Methods:**
- **Series Connection:** Increases voltage, current capacity remains same
- **Parallel Connection:** Increases current capacity, voltage remains same

**Practical Examples:**
- 9V battery contains 6 cells of 1.5V each in series
- Car battery contains 6 lead-acid cells of 2V each in series (12V total)
- Laptop battery may contain multiple lithium-ion cells in series-parallel combination

### Problem 1: Hair Dryer Resistance Calculation

**Given:**
- Resistance of hair dryer = 80 Ω
- Current flowing through resistance = 2.2 A

**Find:** Voltage across the resistance

**Solution:**
Using Ohm's Law: V = I × R

V = 2.2 A × 80 Ω
V = 176 V

**Answer:** The voltage across the hair dryer is 176 V.

### Problem 2: Electrical Appliance Resistance

**Given:**
- EMF source voltage = 10.0 V
- Electric current = 1.5 A
- Conducting wires are resistance-free

**Find:** Resistance offered by the electrical appliance

**Solution:**
Using Ohm's Law: R = V ÷ I

R = 10.0 V ÷ 1.5 A
R = 6.67 Ω

**Answer:** The resistance offered by the electrical appliance is 6.67 Ω.

### Problem 3: Electrical Power Calculation

**Given:**
- Current = 3.5 A
- Voltage = 9 V

**Find:** Electrical power

**Solution:**
Using Power Formula: P = V × I

P = 9 V × 3.5 A
P = 31.5 watts

**Answer:** The electrical power is 31.5 watts.

### Problem 4: Power with Resistance and Current

**Given:**
- Resistance = 6 Ω
- Current = 4.5 A

**Find:** Power of the electrical circuit

**Solution:**
Using Power Formula: P = I² × R

P = (4.5 A)² × 6 Ω
P = 20.25 × 6
P = 121.5 watts

**Answer:** The power of the electrical circuit is 121.5 watts.

### Problem 5: Power with Resistance and Voltage

**Given:**
- Resistance = 7 Ω
- Voltage = 20 V

**Find:** Power of the electrical circuit

**Solution:**
Using Power Formula: P = V² ÷ R

P = (20 V)² ÷ 7 Ω
P = 400 ÷ 7
P = 57.14 watts

**Answer:** The power of the electrical circuit is 57.14 watts.

### Extra Problem 1: Series Circuit Analysis

**Given:** Three resistors in series: R₁ = 10 Ω, R₂ = 20 Ω, R₃ = 30 Ω
Connected to 12V battery

**Find:** Total resistance, current, and voltage across each resistor

**Solution:**
Total Resistance: R_total = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ = 10 + 20 + 30 = 60 Ω

Current: I = V ÷ R_total = 12V ÷ 60Ω = 0.2 A

Voltage across each resistor:
- V₁ = I × R₁ = 0.2 × 10 = 2V
- V₂ = I × R₂ = 0.2 × 20 = 4V  
- V₃ = I × R₃ = 0.2 × 30 = 6V

### Extra Problem 2: Battery Life Calculation

**Given:** Battery capacity = 2000 mAh, Device current = 250 mA

**Find:** How long will the battery last?

**Solution:**
Battery Life = Capacity ÷ Current
Battery Life = 2000 mAh ÷ 250 mA = 8 hours

**Answer:** The battery will last 8 hours.

### Extra Problem 3: Power Consumption Cost

**Given:** 100W bulb runs 5 hours daily for 30 days
Electricity cost = Rs. 10 per kWh

**Find:** Monthly electricity cost for the bulb

**Solution:**
Daily energy = 100W × 5h = 500 Wh = 0.5 kWh
Monthly energy = 0.5 kWh × 30 days = 15 kWh
Monthly cost = 15 kWh × Rs. 10 = Rs. 150

**Answer:** Monthly electricity cost is Rs. 150.

## Key Formulas Summary

**Ohm's Law:**
- V = I × R (Voltage = Current × Resistance)
- I = V ÷ R (Current = Voltage ÷ Resistance)
- R = V ÷ I (Resistance = Voltage ÷ Current)

**Power Formulas:**
- P = V × I (Power = Voltage × Current)
- P = I² × R (Power = Current² × Resistance)
- P = V² ÷ R (Power = Voltage² ÷ Resistance)

**Energy Formula:**
- E = P × t (Energy = Power × Time)

**Battery Life:**
- Life = Capacity ÷ Current Draw

**Series Circuit:**
- R_total = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ...
- I is same throughout
- V_total = V₁ + V₂ + V₃ + ...

**Parallel Circuit:**
- 1/R_total = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + ...
- V is same across all branches
- I_total = I₁ + I₂ + I₃ + ...
