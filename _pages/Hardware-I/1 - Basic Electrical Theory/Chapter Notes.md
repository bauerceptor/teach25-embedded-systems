# Chapter 1: Basic Electrical Theory

*Note: The historical background and development details in this chapter are provided for better understanding and are not part of the exam syllabus.*

## Historical Development of Electrical Science

The understanding of electricity has evolved over centuries through the work of many scientists. Benjamin Franklin first identified positive and negative charges in the 1740s. Alessandro Volta invented the first battery in 1800, providing a steady source of electric current. Michael Faraday discovered electromagnetic induction in 1831, which became the basis for electric generators and motors. These discoveries laid the foundation for our modern electrical systems.

## 1.1 Basic Principle of Electricity

### 1.1.1 Electricity

Electricity is the flow of electrical energy. Electricity is the secondary form of energy which means that other sources of energy such as kinetic energy, thermal energy and solar energy are converted into electrical energy.

The process of electricity generation involves converting various forms of primary energy into electrical energy. In hydroelectric dams, the kinetic energy of flowing water rotates turbines connected to generators. In thermal power plants, coal is burned to produce heat, which converts water into steam. This steam drives turbines that generate electricity. Solar panels convert light energy directly into electrical energy through the photovoltaic effect.

### How Electricity Reaches Your Home

Electricity is generated at dams and thermal power plants located in remote areas. The electricity that power plants generate is delivered to consumers over transmission and distribution power lines. This complex system is sometimes called the "grid" and includes substations, transformers, and power lines that connect electricity producers and consumers.

The electrical grid operates at different voltage levels for efficiency. Power plants generate electricity at around 25,000 volts. This voltage is stepped up to 138,000-765,000 volts for long-distance transmission to reduce energy losses. Near communities, substations step the voltage down to 4,000-35,000 volts for local distribution. Finally, transformers on utility poles or in underground systems reduce the voltage to 120-240 volts for household use.

## 1.1.2 Electrical Quantities and Units

Electricity is represented by electrical quantities. There are three main electrical quantities:

1. Voltage
2. Current  
3. Resistance

### Voltage

A voltage is a potential difference between two points of an electric circuit. A voltage is the pressure from an electrical power source that causes current in an electric circuit. A voltage is like a diffusion phenomenon. A voltage is represented by V and is measured in volts (V).

Think of voltage as similar to water pressure in a pipe system. Just as higher water pressure pushes more water through pipes, higher voltage pushes more electric current through circuits. The voltage creates the "electrical pressure" that makes electrons move through conductors.

**Real-world examples:**
- A standard household outlet provides 120V or 240V
- Car batteries provide 12V  
- Mobile phone batteries provide 3.7V
- High-voltage power lines carry 138,000V or more

### Current

Flow of charges inside an electric circuit is called electric current. An electric power source such as a battery consists of high number of charged particles which creates a potential difference between two points of an electric circuit, causing current. A current is represented by I and is measured in amperes (A).

Electric current is the movement of electrons through a conductor. When a voltage is applied across a conductor, it creates an electric field that causes free electrons to drift in one direction. This drift of electrons constitutes electric current.

**Real-world examples:**
- A typical household light bulb draws 0.5A to 1A
- Mobile phone chargers provide 1A to 3A  
- Electric car charging can require 50A to 200A
- Lightning carries approximately 30,000A

### Resistance

A resistance is a component opposing the current flow in an electric circuit. The higher the value of resistance, more will be the opposition. A resistance is represented by R and is measured in ohms (Ω).

Resistance occurs because electrons collide with atoms as they move through a material. Different materials have different levels of resistance based on their atomic structure and the availability of free electrons.

## 1.1.3 AC and DC Quantities

There are two types of current: Direct Current (DC) and Alternating Current (AC). Magnitude and direction of DC remains constant while AC keeps on changing its direction. Similarly, there exists AC and DC voltages.

**Direct Current (DC):**
- Current flows in one direction only
- Voltage remains constant over time
- Examples: batteries, solar panels, electronic devices

**Alternating Current (AC):**  
- Current changes direction periodically
- Voltage varies sinusoidally over time
- Examples: household electricity, power grid

The reason both AC and DC exist is due to their different advantages. DC is easier to store in batteries and is preferred for electronic devices. AC is more efficient for long-distance transmission and can be easily transformed to different voltage levels using transformers.

## 1.1.4 Conductors, Insulators and Semiconductors

### Conductors

Current can flow in conductors. Conductors have free electrons on their surface which allow current to pass through easily. They are made up of atoms having free electrons. Copper, silver and aluminum are good conductors of electricity.

In conductors, the outermost electrons are loosely bound to their atoms and can move freely throughout the material. This creates a "sea of electrons" that can carry electric current easily.

**Common conductors:**
- Copper: Used in electrical wiring
- Aluminum: Used in power transmission lines  
- Silver: Used in high-quality electrical connections
- Gold: Used in computer circuits (does not corrode)

### Insulators

Insulators are made up of material that do not allow electricity to pass through it. They are made up of atoms that do not have free electrons. Plastic, glass and rubber are good insulators for electricity.

In insulators, electrons are tightly bound to their atoms and cannot move freely. This prevents the flow of electric current through the material.

**Common insulators:**
- Plastic: Used for wire coating and electrical components
- Glass: Used in power line insulators
- Rubber: Used in electrical gloves and mats
- Ceramic: Used in electrical insulators
- Air: Acts as an insulator in most conditions

### Semiconductors

Semiconductors have limited free electrons so their conductivity lies between conductors and insulators. Silicon and germanium are semiconductors.

Semiconductors are unique because their conductivity can be controlled by adding impurities (doping) or by applying voltage. This property makes them essential for electronic devices like transistors, diodes, and computer chips.

**Applications of semiconductors:**
- Computer processors
- Solar cells
- LED lights
- Radio and television circuits

## 1.1.5 Electrical Power and Energy

### Electric Power

Electric power of an electric circuit or appliance is the rate at which it consumes energy. Unit of Power is Watt which is 1 Joule per second.

**Power Formula:**
P = ΔE/Δt  (Equation 1)

Since voltage is work done per unit charge and current is charge per unit time:
P = (W/Q) × (Q/t) = W/t

Therefore: **P = VI**  (Equation 3)

**Real-world examples:**
- A 60W light bulb consumes 60 watts of power
- A microwave oven typically uses 1000W (1kW)
- An electric car motor can use 50kW to 200kW

### Electric Energy

It is a form of energy resulting from flow of electrons. It is usually derived from power. Most common unit of consuming energy is in terms of kWh, that is equivalent of using 1 kilowatt power for one hour. kWh is also used as a unit for computing electricity bill by energy suppliers.

Energy consumed = Power × Time
E = P × t

If power is in kilowatts and time is in hours, energy is in kilowatt-hours (kWh).

**Example:** A 100W light bulb running for 10 hours consumes:
E = 0.1 kW × 10 h = 1 kWh of energy

## 1.1.6 Instruments to Measure Electrical Quantities

### Ohm Meter

An ohm meter is a device to measure resistance. Connecting leads of ohm meter are connected with the resistance to measure and display the resistance on ohm meter.

**How to use an ohm meter:**
1. Turn off power to the circuit
2. Connect the test leads to both ends of the resistor
3. Read the resistance value on the display

### Voltmeter

A voltmeter is used to measure voltages. Probes of voltmeter are connected with the terminals of the circuits across which voltages are to be measured. A voltmeter is always connected in parallel across a component whose voltages are to be measured.

**Key points about voltmeters:**
- Always connected in parallel
- Has very high internal resistance
- Measures potential difference between two points

### Ammeter

An ammeter is used to measure current. Probes of ammeter are connected with the terminals of the circuits across which current is to be measured. It should be noted that ammeter is always connected in series in the circuit whose current is to be measured.

**Key points about ammeters:**
- Always connected in series
- Has very low internal resistance
- Measures current flowing through the circuit

### Digital Multimeter (DMM)

A digital multimeter is an instrument to measure different electrical quantities. Digital multimeters combine the testing capabilities of single-task meters: the voltmeter (for measuring volts), ammeter (amps) and ohmmeter (ohms).

### Oscilloscope

An oscilloscope is a laboratory instrument commonly used to display and analyze the waveform of electronic signals. It draws a graph of the instantaneous signal voltage in terms of time.

**Oscilloscope controls:**
- Voltage/div: Controls the scale of the y-axis
- Time/div: Controls the scale of the x-axis  
- Multiple channels: Can display several signals simultaneously
- Trigger: Synchronizes the display with the input signal

## 1.2 Ohm's Law

Ohm's law states that the current flowing in a circuit is directly proportional to the voltage across it, given that all the physical quantities and temperature remains the same.

**V ∝ I**  (Equation 4)

Introducing a constant of proportionality:
**V = IR**  (Equation 5)

This is the fundamental relationship between voltage, current, and resistance.

**From this equation, we can derive:**
- V = I × R (to find voltage)
- I = V ÷ R (to find current)  
- R = V ÷ I (to find resistance)

### Calculating Electrical Power Using Ohm's Law

Ohm's Law can be used to rewrite the power formula. The power formula written in terms of Ohm's law is:

**P = (IR) × I = I²R**  (Equation 6)

or

**P = V × (V/R) = V²/R**  (Equation 7)

These equations allow us to calculate power when we know different combinations of voltage, current, and resistance.

## 1.3 Cells and Batteries

### Cell

A cell is a single unit device that converts chemical energy into electrical energy. Typical rating of a cell is 1.5V while 3.7V is also available. A cell can be rechargeable or non-rechargeable.

### Battery

A battery is a group of cells connected together. Cells can be connected in series or parallel. Polarity of cells must be taken care of while forming a battery.

### Series and Parallel Connection of Cells/Batteries

**Series Connection:** A series connection is formed when positive terminal of one battery is connected with the negative terminal of the other battery. Series connection increases the voltage capacity of the battery bank but current capacity remains the same.

**Parallel Connection:** A parallel connection is formed when positive terminals are connected with positive terminals of other batteries and negative terminals are also connected together. Parallel connection increases the current capacity of the battery bank while voltage rating remains the same.

### Importance of Batteries

- A battery provides portability to a device or appliance
- The main purpose of the batteries is to store electrical energy
- Rechargeable batteries enable multiple times usage of batteries
- Size of battery is very crucial to use it in a specific application

## 1.3.1 Types of Batteries

There are four main types of secondary batteries:

1. **Lead Acid Batteries**
2. **Nickel Cadmium Batteries**  
3. **Nickel Metal Hydride Batteries**
4. **Lithium Ion Batteries**

### Lead Acid Batteries

Lead-acid batteries are the most widely used batteries. They have 70 to 80% conversion efficiency. These batteries are mostly used in UPS and vehicles.

**Characteristics:**
- Voltage per cell: 2V
- Heavy but reliable
- Low cost
- Good for high current applications

### Nickel Cadmium Batteries

Ni-Cd batteries use Nickel Oxyhydroxide (NiOOH) as Cathode and Cadmium metal (Cd) as anode. These batteries usually have a voltage rating of 1.2V. These batteries are mostly used in photography equipment and power tools.

### Nickel Metal Hydride Batteries

These are relatively new type of batteries are an extended version of Nickel Hydrogen Electrode batteries, which were exclusively used in aerospace applications (satellites). The positive electrode is the Nickel Oxyhydroxide (NiOOH) while the negative electrode of the cell is a metal alloy, where hydrogen is stored reversibly.

### Lithium Ion Batteries

A lithium-ion battery is a family of rechargeable battery types in which lithium ions move from the negative electrode to the positive electrode during discharge and back when charging. They have the best energy to weight ratios.

**Advantages of Lithium-ion batteries:**
- High energy density
- Low self-discharge
- No memory effect
- Lightweight

## 1.3.2 Components of a Battery

Cells are comprised of 3 essential components:

1. **The Anode** is the negative or reducing electrode that releases electrons to the external circuit and oxidizes during an electrochemical reaction.

2. **The Cathode** is the positive or oxidizing electrode that acquires electrons from the external circuit and is reduced during the electrochemical reaction.

3. **The Electrolyte** is the medium that provides the ion transport mechanism between the cathode and anode of a cell. Electrolytes are often thought of as liquids, such as water or other solvents, with dissolved salts, acids, or alkalis that are required for ionic conduction.

## 1.3.3 Working Principle of a Battery

A battery works on the oxidation and reduction reaction of an electrolyte with metals. When two dissimilar metallic substances, called electrodes, are placed in a diluted electrolyte, oxidation and reduction reactions take place in the electrodes respectively depending upon the electron affinity of the metal of the electrodes.

**The process works as follows:**

1. Two different metals are immersed in an electrolyte solution
2. The metal with low electron affinity gains electrons from negative ions
3. The metal with high electron affinity releases electrons to positive ions  
4. This creates a difference in electron concentration between the metals
5. The difference in electron concentration creates an electrical potential difference
6. This potential difference can be used as a voltage source

**Key concepts:**
- **Electrolyte:** A compound that dissolves in water to produce positive and negative ions
- **Electron affinity:** The energy released when a neutral atom accepts an electron
- **EMF (Electromotive Force):** The electrical potential difference generated by the battery

## 1.3.4 Charging Principle of a Battery

During charging of a battery, external DC source is applied to the battery. The negative terminal of the DC source is connected to the negative plate or anode of the battery and positive terminal of the source is connected to the positive plate or cathode of the battery.

**Charging process:**
1. External DC voltage is applied to reverse the discharge process
2. Electrons are injected into the anode from the external source
3. Reduction reaction takes place in the anode instead of cathode
4. The anode regains electrons and returns to its original state
5. Oxidation reaction takes place at the cathode
6. The cathode material regains its original chemical composition

## 1.3.5 Maintaining a Battery

Maintenance increases battery life. Follow the following procedures for better maintaining a battery:

- Maintain electrolyte level of a battery
- Regularly clean the terminals of a battery
- Battery should be charged slowly
- Allow the battery to cool down once it is fully charged

## 1.3.6 Testing of a Battery

### Visual Test

- Check the container, cover and terminals. If physical damage is present, replace the battery
- Check the indicator (If the battery has the indicator). Always look right down when viewing the indicator and lightly tap the indicator on the battery to dislodge any air bubbles

### Voltage Test

- If voltage is below 12.4V, recharge the battery immediately

### Load Test

- Connect the battery tester to battery terminals
- Measure the temperature of the battery around  
- Apply the load for 15 seconds and read the voltage
- If the voltages drop significantly, recharge the battery and test again
- If the battery fails the load test twice, replace it

### Gravity Test

The most accurate and direct way to test the state of charge of a battery cell is to determine the specific gravity of the battery electrolyte. The higher the specific gravity of the electrolyte the higher the state of charge.

**Hydrometer** is used to perform gravity test. A good hydrometer is accurate to +/- 0.005 points.

## 1.3.7 Battery Life

A 10 Ah battery delivering 1A, would last 10 hours. If delivering 10A, it would last for only 1 hour, or if delivering 5A, it would last only for 2 hours.

**Battery life calculation:**
Battery Life = Battery Capacity ÷ Current Draw

**Example:** For a 18650 2500mAh (2.5Ah) battery with a device that draws 500mA (0.5A):
Battery Life = 2.5Ah ÷ 0.5A = 5 Hours

**Basic Electrical Quantities:**
- Electricity is a secondary form of energy converted from other energy sources
- Voltage is the electrical pressure that drives current through circuits
- Current is the flow of electric charges through a conductor

**Types of Current:**
- DC (Direct Current): constant magnitude and direction

- Conductors allow easy current flow due to free electrons
- Insulators prevent current flow due to lack of free electrons
- Semiconductors have controllable conductivity between conductors and insulators

- Energy is power consumed over time (measured in kWh for billing)

- Ohm meter measures resistance
- Voltmeter measures voltage (connected in parallel)

- V = IR is the fundamental relationship between voltage, current, and resistance
- Power can be calculated using P = VI, P = I²R, or P = V²/R

- Batteries convert chemical energy to electrical energy
- Series connection increases voltage, parallel connection increases current capacity
- Different battery types (Lead-acid, Ni-Cd, Ni-MH, Li-ion) have different characteristics
- Proper maintenance and testing ensure battery reliability

These fundamental concepts are essential for understanding more advanced electrical and electronic systems used in IoT applications. The relationship between voltage, current, resistance, and power governs the behavior of all electrical circuits and devices.
